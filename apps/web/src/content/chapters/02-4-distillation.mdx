Massive frontier models (with hundreds of billions or trillions of parameters) have incredible capabilities, but they are slow and expensive to run in production. Model distillation is the process of transferring that capability into smaller, faster models.

## Quick Summary

- Distillation transfers knowledge from a large "teacher" model to a smaller "student" model.
- The student learns by matching the raw probability distributions (logits) of the teacher.
- A targeted, fine-tuned 8B model can match or exceed a generic 70B model on narrow tasks.

---

## The Teacher-Student Framework

In knowledge distillation, we have two models:
1. **The Teacher**: A large, highly capable model (e.g. 405 Billion parameters) that has already been trained.
2. **The Student**: A small model (e.g. 8 Billion parameters) that we want to train.

Instead of training the student only on binary right/wrong labels, we train it to replicate the **entire output probability distribution** of the teacher. 

For example, when asked a question, the teacher might output:
* `"Paris"`: 85%
* `"France"`: 10%
* `"Europe"`: 4%
* `"Banana"`: 0.0001%

The student model gets trained to match these exact percentages. This is known as **soft target matching**. Because the percentages convey the teacher's internal structure (knowing that "France" is a close alternative to "Paris", but "Banana" is completely wrong), the student learns much faster and more deeply than it would from reading raw text alone.

## Task Matchups: Big vs Small Models

Why do we care so much about small models? In real-world engineering, speed (latency) and hosting cost are critical constraints:

* **Vanilla 70B Model**: Knows a broad set of facts, translates 50 languages, and writes poetry. But it is slow and requires multiple high-end GPUs.
* **Fine-Tuned 8B Model**: Frozen on everything else, but trained deeply on one specific task (e.g., converting emails to JSON schemas). On that specific task, it will often beat the 70B model while running 10x faster and at a fraction of the cost.

---

## Remember

<RememberCard>
  - Distillation uses a large model's logits to guide a smaller model's training.
  - Soft target matching preserves the semantic relationships between wrong options.
  - Fine-tuning smaller models for narrow tasks is a key cost-reduction strategy.
</RememberCard>
